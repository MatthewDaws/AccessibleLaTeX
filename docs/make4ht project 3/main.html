1 Introduction

The classical Bohr Compactification of a topological (semi)group can be defined in terms of unitary representations (see the original paper of von Neumann [29] or see [2, ???] for a modern treatment). Recently, see [26], Sołtan has extended this idea to give a definition of a compactification of a quantum semigroup, a process which yields a compact quantum group, in the sense of Woronowicz (see [30]).

For a locally compact group G, we can describe the Bohr (or equivalently almost periodic) compactification of G in terms of a certain function space on G. Indeed, we can describe this function space in terms of the Banach algebra L1(G) and its dual L∞(G). We turn the dual into an L1(G)-bimodule in the usual way, and then define f ∈ L∞(G) to be almost periodic if the map

Rf : L1(G) → L∞(G); a↦a ⋅ f(a ∈ L1(G))

is compact. If we let AP ⁡ (G) ⊆ L∞(G) be the collection of almost periodic elements, then each member of AP ⁡ (G) is continuous; AP ⁡ (G) is a unital C∗-subalgebra of L∞(G); if the character space of AP ⁡ (G) is GAP ⁡ , then GAP ⁡ becomes a compact group containing a dense homeomorphic image of G.

For an abelian locally compact group G, we have the Fourier transform L1(G) → C 0(Ĝ), where Ĝ is the dual group to G. The image is defined as the Fourier algebra of Ĝ, see [4, ???]. For a non-abelian group G, Eymard defined an commutative Banach algebra of functions on G, written A(G), which extends the definition of the Fourier algebra, see [8]. As the definition of almost periodic above makes sense for any Banach algebra, it is not surprising that the space of almost periodic functionals on the Fourier algebra has been studied. The definition appears to have been made in [5, Section 7], where the authors write AP ⁡ (Ĝ), in analogy with the abelian case. Further study was undertaken in [9] and [14], for example. An excellent survey (which we use extensively below) is Chou’s paper [3]. See also [10] and [18] for more recent work.

The space AP ⁡ (Ĝ) is not nearly as well understood as AP ⁡ (G), and it appears that no study has been undertaken of AP ⁡ (Ĝ) in the context of a categorical notion of “compactification”. The aim of this paper is to undertake such a study.

2 Compact quantum group compactifications

We shall quickly sketch Sołtan’s work in [26]. Following Sołtan, we shall define a compact quantum semigroup to be a pair 𝕊 = (𝒜, Φ) where 𝒜 is a unital C∗-algebra and Φ : 𝒜→𝒜⊗𝒜 is a co-associative product. Here, and henceforth, 𝒜⊗𝒜 is the minimal or spacial tensor product of 𝒜 with itself. To say that Φ is a co-associative product means that Φ is a unital ∗-homomorphism and (Φ ⊗ id ⁡ )Φ = (id ⁡ ⊗Φ)Φ.

A compact quantum group is a compact quantum semigroup 𝔾 = (𝒜, Φ) such that the linear span of the either of the sets {(a ⊗ I)Φ(b) : a,b ∈𝒜} or {(I ⊗ a)Φ(b) : a,b ∈𝒜} is dense in 𝒜⊗𝒜. These density conditions imply a large amount of structure: see [30] or the survey [16] for further details. The density conditions can actually be weakened: see [17].

Compact quantum groups have a very rich (co)representation theory. For example, every irreducible corepresentation is finite-dimensional. As in the classical case, Søltan’s quantum compactification is stated in terms of finite-dimensional corepresentations. We first need the notion of a quantum semigroup. This is a pair 𝕊 = (𝒜, Φ) where 𝒜 is a C∗-algebra (now not assumed unital) and Φ : 𝒜→ M(𝒜⊗𝒜) is a co-associative, non-degenerate ∗-homomorphism. Here M(𝒜⊗𝒜) is the multiplier algebra of 𝒜⊗𝒜. See [13], for example, for further details about multiplier algebras. Recall that to say that Φ is non-degenerate means that Φ(𝒜)(𝒜⊗𝒜) is dense in 𝒜⊗𝒜. As such, there is a unique extension of Φ to a ∗-homomorphism M(𝒜) → M(𝒜⊗𝒜). Similarly, Φ ⊗ id ⁡ and id ⁡ ⊗Φ make sense as maps M(𝒜⊗𝒜) → M(𝒜⊗𝒜⊗𝒜), and so it makes sense to speak of Φ as being co-associative.

We now state the definitions which Søltan makes in [26]. However, we shall state them in different ways which are more compatible with our Banach algebra approach. Fix a quantum semigroup 𝕊 = (𝒜, Φ). In the usual way, we use Φ to induce an associative product on 𝒜′, turning 𝒜′ into a Banach algebra, by

⟨μλ,a⟩ = ⟨μ ⊗ λ, Φ(a)⟩(a ∈𝒜,μ,λ ∈𝒜′).

Let X be a finite-dimensional Banach space, and let T ∈ℬ(X) ⊗ M(𝒜). For each μ ∈𝒜′, let Tμ ∈ℬ(X) be the slice map, Tμ = (id ⁡ ⊗μ)T. We say that T is a bounded representation if T is invertible in the algebra ℬ(X) ⊗ M(𝒜), and the map 𝒜′ →ℬ(X),μ↦T μ is an algebra homomorphism. Notice that we always have the trivial representation, IX ⊗ I𝒜. It is easy to construct direct sums of bounded representations, and with a little more effort, tensor products, see [26, Section 2.1].

Let ad ⁡ : ℬ(X) →ℬ(X′),S↦S′ be the adjoint map. For T ∈ℬ(X) ⊗ M(𝒜), let TT = (ad ⁡ ⊗ id ⁡ )T be the transpose of T. Then a bounded representation T is admissible if TT is invertible in ℬ(X′) ⊗ M(𝒜).

When H is a (finite-dimensional) Hilbert space, ℬ(H) ⊗ M(𝒜) becomes a C∗-algebra. When a bounded representation T ∈ℬ(H) ⊗ M(𝒜) is a unitary, we say that T is a unitary representation. We say that a bounded representation T ∈ℬ(X) ⊗ M(𝒜) is similar to a unitary representation if X admits a Hilbert space structure turning T into a unitary representation. Then Søltan shows that admissible representations are similar to unitary representations; the converse need not hold.

Let T ∈ℬ(X) ⊗ M(𝒜) be an admissible representation, and let λ ∈ℬ(X)′. Then (λ ⊗ id ⁡ )T ∈ M(𝒜) is a matrix element of T. Søltan shows that the linear span of matrix elements of all admissible representations is a unital ∗-subalgebra of M(𝒜), see [26, Proposition 2.12]. Let 𝔸ℙ(𝕊) be the closure of this algebra, so 𝔸ℙ(𝕊) ⊆ M(𝒜) is a unital C∗-algebra. Let Δ𝔸ℙ(𝕊) be the restriction of Φ to 𝔸ℙ(𝕊). Then Δ𝔸ℙ(𝕊) is a ∗-homomorphism from 𝔸ℙ(𝕊) to 𝔸ℙ(𝕊) ⊗𝔸ℙ(𝕊), and the pair 𝔟𝕊 = (𝔸ℙ(𝕊), Δ𝔸ℙ(𝕊)) satisfies the density conditions to become a compact quantum group.

The compact quantum group 𝔟𝕊 satisfies the expected universal property. Let χ𝕊 : 𝔸ℙ(𝕊) → M(𝒜) be the inclusion map. Then, if 𝔾 = (ℬ, Φ

B)isacompactquantumgroup,and𝜃 : ℬ→ M(𝒜) is a non-degenerate1 ∗-homomorphism such that Φ ∘ 𝜃 = (𝜃 ⊗ 𝜃) ∘ Φℬ, there is a unique ∗-homomorphism 𝔟𝜃 : ℬ→𝔸ℙ(𝕊) such that χ𝕊 ∘𝔟𝜃 = 𝜃.

If S is a topological semigroup, ℬ = C0(S), and Φ is the canonical dualisation of the product on S, then 𝔸ℙ(𝕊) is isomorphic to C(SAP ⁡ ), where SAP ⁡ is the classical Bohr compactification of S.

2.1 For the Fourier algebra

We now turn our attention to the Fourier algebra A(G). Let G be a locally compact group. Let λ : G →ℬ(L2(G)) be the left-regular representation

(λ(s)f)(t) = f(s−1t)(f ∈ L2(G),s,t ∈ G).

By integrating, we get a contractive homomorphism λ : L1(G) →ℬ(L2(G)). The norm closure of λ(L1(G)) is Cr∗(G), the reduced group C∗-algebra of G. If we take the weak-operator topology closure, then we get V N(G), the group von Neumann algebra. Equivalently, V N(G) is the von Neumann algebra generated by {λ(s) : s ∈ G}. The predual of V N(G) is A(G), the Fourier algebra of G. This algebra is in standard position on L2(G), and so, for each a ∈ A(G), there exists f,g ∈ L2(G) such that

⟨T,a⟩ = (Tf|g)(T ∈ V N(G)).

We can treat a as a member of C0(G) by

a(s) = ⟨λ(s),a⟩ = (λ(s)f|g) = ∫ Gf(s−1t)g(t)¯dt(s ∈ G).

We identify L2(G) ⊗ L2(G) with L2(G × G). Define a unitary W ∈ℬ(L2(G × G) by

(Wf)(s,t) = f(s,st)(s,t ∈ G,f ∈ L2(G × G)).

Then we may define a map Δ : V N(G) → V N(G)⊗¯V N(G) = V N(G × G) by

Δ(T) = W∗(T ⊗ I)W(T ∈ V N(G)).

We can check that Δ(λ(s)) = λ(s) ⊗ λ(s) = λ(s,s) for s ∈ G. Then Δ is a normal ∗-homomorphism which, in the appropriate sense, is co-associative. The pre-adjoint of Δ turns A(G) into a Banach algebra, and we can check that A(G) becomes a (not closed) subalgebra of C0(G).

There are lots of ways to define the multiplier algebra of a C∗-algebra. For us, the most useful approach shall be to follow Pedersen, [19, Section 3.12]. If 𝒜 is a C∗-algebra inside ℬ(H), and ℳ⊆ℬ(H) is the von Neumann algebra generated by 𝒜, then we can identify M(𝒜) with

{x ∈ℳ : ax,xa ∈𝒜(a ∈𝒜)}.

Using this, we can identify M(Cr∗(G)) as a ∗-subalgebra of V N(G). By the definition of the spacial tensor product, we identify Cr∗(G) ⊗ C r∗(G) with a ∗-subalgebra of V N(G)⊗¯V N(G) = V N(G × G). Hence, again, we identify M(Cr∗(G) ⊗ C r∗(G)) with a ∗-subalgebra of V N(G × G). We can then show that Δ restricts to give a coassociative, non-degenerate ∗-homomorphism Cr∗(G) → M(C r∗(G) ⊗ C r∗(G)), and so 𝔾 = (Cr∗(G), Δ) is a quantum semigroup (which obviously also has extra structure). It is simple to see that λ(s) ∈ M(Cr∗(G)) for each s ∈ G. For further details, see [13, Chapter 8], although be aware that Lance looks at the right-regular representation.

Following Chou, [3], we define Trig ⁡ (Ĝ) to be the linear span of {λ(s) : s ∈ G} in M(Cr∗(G)), and let Cδ∗(G) be the closure.

Proposition 2.1. We have that Δ restricts to Cδ∗(G) to give a ∗-homomorphism Cδ∗(G) → C δ∗(G) ⊗ C δ∗(G), turning Cδ∗(G) into a compact quantum group.

Proof. By the definition of the spacial tensor norm, it is clear that Cδ∗(G) ⊗ C δ∗(G) is the closure of Trig ⁡ (Ĝ) ⊗Trig ⁡ (Ĝ) inside V N(G)⊗¯V N(G) = V N(G × G). It is hence easy to see that Cδ∗(G) ⊗ C δ∗(G) can be identified with Cδ∗(G × G), from which it follows that Δ does map Cδ∗(G) into Cδ∗(G) ⊗ C δ∗(G).

Notice that

(λ(st−1) ⊗ I)Δ(λ(t)) = (λ(st−1) ⊗ I)(λ(t) ⊗ λ(t)) = λ(s) ⊗ λ(t)(s,t ∈ G).

It hence follows that {(a ⊗ I)Δ(b) : a,b ∈ Cδ∗(G)} is linearly dense in Cδ∗(G) ⊗ C δ∗(G) = C δ∗(G × G). Similarly, {(I ⊗ a)Δ(b) : a,b ∈ Cδ∗(G)} is linearly dense in Cδ∗(G) ⊗ C δ∗(G), showing that Cδ∗(G) is a compact quantum group. □

We remark that the Haar state h on Cδ∗(G) is simply the map given by h(λ(s)) = 1 if s = eG the identity of G, and 0 otherwise. Let Gd be the group G with the discrete topology. If Gd is amenable, then we know that Cr∗(G d) = C∗(G d), and so it follows that Cδ∗(G) is isomorphic to Cr∗(G d), and in particular, the Haar state is faithful. In general, it seems possible that the Haar state could fail to be faithful. 2

Theorem 2.2. Let 𝔾 = (Cr∗(G), Δ) be as above. Then 𝔟𝔾 = (Cδ∗(G), Δ).

Proof. By the universal property, we have that Cδ∗(G) ⊆𝔸ℙ(𝔾). We follow Søltan’s construction of 𝔸ℙ(𝔾); in particular, we shall show that if T ∈ℬ(H) ⊗ M(Cr∗(G)) is a unitary representation, then each matrix element of T is a member of Trig ⁡ (Ĝ). This shows that 𝔸ℙ(𝔾) ⊆ Cδ∗(G), as required.

If H is n-dimensional, then we can identify ℬ(H) with 𝕄n, and so identify ℬ(H) ⊗ M(Cr∗(G)) with 𝕄n(M(Cr∗(G)). Let T = (Tij)1≤i,j≤n. Then T being a representation implies that

Δ(Tij) = ∑ k=1nT ik ⊗ Tkj(1 ≤ i,j ≤ n),

see before Lemma 2.6 in [26]. Again, we identify M(Cr∗(G)) with a ∗-subalgebra of V N(G). Now, V N(G) becomes an A(G)-module, and we have that

⟨a ⋅ T,b⟩ = ⟨T,ab⟩ = ⟨Δ(T),a ⊗ b⟩(a,b ∈ A(G),T ∈ V N(G)).

Hence

a ⋅ Tij = ∑ k=1n⟨T ik,a⟩Tkj(1 ≤ i,j ≤ n,a ∈ A(G)),

showing in particular that the map A(G) → V N(G),a↦a ⋅ Tij is finite-rank. By Chou’s work, [3, Proposition 2.3], this implies that Tij ∈Trig ⁡ (Ĝ). It is easy to see (compare [26, Lemma 2.6]) that the set of matrix elements of T agrees with the linear span of {Tij : 1 ≤ i,j ≤ n}, so we conclude that each matrix element of T is a member of Trig ⁡ (Ĝ), as required. □

Instead of working with Cr∗(G), one could start with the full C∗-algebra C∗(G). Søltan shows that 𝔸ℙ(C∗(G)) is also equal to the closure of the span of {λ(s) : s ∈ G}, but this time inside M(C∗(G)).

The following tells us that, informally, there is no “bigger” compact quantum group sitting inside V N(G) than Cδ∗(G).

Corollary 2.3. Let G be a locally compact group, and let 𝒜⊆ V N(G) be a unital ∗-subalgebra such that Δ restricts to give a map 𝒜→𝒜⊗𝒜, making (𝒜, Δ) into a compact quantum group. Then 𝒜⊆ Cδ∗(G).

Proof. Let A be the subspace of 𝒜 formed by taking the linear span of matrix elements of finite-dimensional, unitary representations of 𝒜. By [30, Theorem 2.2] we know that A is a dense ∗-subalgebra of 𝒜. Examining the above proof, we see immediately that A ⊆Trig ⁡ (Ĝ), from which it follows that 𝒜⊆ Cδ∗(G). □

If we replaced V N(G) by M(Cr∗(G)), then the above corollary would immediately follow from the definition of 𝔟Cr∗(G) as a universal object behaving as a compactification.

3 Almost periodic elements

Let 𝒜 be a Banach algebra, and turn 𝒜′ into an 𝒜-bimodule in the usual way,

⟨a ⋅ μ,b⟩ = ⟨μ,ba⟩,πμ ⋅ ab = ⟨μ,ab⟩(a,b ∈𝒜,μ ∈𝒜′).

For μ ∈𝒜′, let ℛμ : 𝒜→𝒜′ be the map ℛμ(a) = a ⋅ μ. When ℛμ is a compact operator, we say that μ is almost periodic, written μ ∈AP ⁡ (𝒜). We can show that AP ⁡ (𝒜) is a closed 𝒜-submodule of 𝒜′.

We similarly turn 𝒜″ ⁡ into an 𝒜-bimodule. We define an action of 𝒜″ ⁡ on 𝒜′ by

⟨Φ ⋅ μ,a⟩ = ⟨Φ,μ ⋅ a⟩,⟨μ ⋅ Φ,a⟩ = ⟨Φ,a ⋅ μ⟩(a ∈𝒜,μ ∈𝒜′, Φ ∈𝒜″ ⁡).

Then we define two bilinear maps □,◊ on 𝒜″ ⁡ by

⟨Φ□Ψ,μ⟩ = ⟨Φ, Ψ ⋅ μ⟩,⟨Φ◊Ψ,μ⟩ = ⟨Ψ,μ ⋅ Φ⟩(Φ, Ψ ∈𝒜″ ⁡,μ ∈𝒜′).

Then □ and ◊ are algebra products, called the Arens products. They both extend the product on 𝒜 in the sense that, for a ∈𝒜 and Φ ∈𝒜″ ⁡, a ⋅ Φ = κmcA(a)□Φ = κ𝒜(a)◊Φ, and similarly on the right. See [4, ???] or [?, ???] for further details.

The following result essentially appears a number of times in the literture, but for our purposes, is well treated by Lau in [14, Theorem 5.8].

Proposition 3.1. Let 𝒜be a Banach algebra, and let X ⊆𝒜′ be a closed 𝒜-submodule. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. X ⊆AP ⁡ (𝒜);
  2. Treating X′ as a quotient of A″ ⁡, both Arens products drop to well-defined algebra products on X′, these products agree, and the resuling product is jointly weak∗-continuous on bounded subsets.

Let G be a locally compact group. We say that f ∈ Cb(G) is almost periodic if the set {fs : s ∈ G} is relatively compact in Cb(G), where fs(t) = f(ts) for t ∈ G. Using bounded approximate identities, it is easy (compare with [28]) to show that AP ⁡ (L1(G)) = AP ⁡ (G). We know that AP ⁡ (G) is a closed ∗-subalgebra of Cb(G), with spectrum GAP ⁡ say. Then G is dense in GAP ⁡ , and extending the product from G to GAP ⁡ turns GAP ⁡ into a compact group. Furthermore, GAP ⁡ then agrees with the Bohr compactification of G. See [2] for further details.

As A(G) can be thought of as being the “dual” to L1(G) (hence extending the abelian case, when A(G) = L1(Ĝ)), it is not surprising that AP ⁡ (A(G)) has been studied. It is customary to write AP ⁡ (Ĝ) for AP ⁡ (A(G)). See the references [5, Section 7], [9], [14], [10], [18], and especially [3] for further details. It would be fair to say that, for a general locally compact group G, the above proposition is about all that is known about AP ⁡ (Ĝ) (though see below for our discussion of invariant states).

When G is abelian, by applying Fourier transforms, we can show that AP ⁡ (Ĝ) = Cδ∗(G) = 𝔟C r∗(G). Similarly, when G is discrete and amenable, we have AP ⁡ (Ĝ) = 𝔟Cr∗(G) (this follows from inspecting [9, Proposition 2]). We do not know, in general, if AP ⁡ (Ĝ) need be a C∗-algebra. Chou defines a group G to have the dual Bohr approximation property if AP ⁡ (Ĝ) = Cδ∗(G) = 𝔟C r∗(G). In [3], and in Rinder’s correction [22], it is shown that there exist compact groups G which fail to have the dual Bohr approximation property. Indeed, the idea is as follows. Let E ∈ V N(G) be the rank-one operator defined by

E(f) = ∫ Gf1G(f ∈ L2(G)),

where 1G is the constant function on G. Then it is possible for both ℛE to be compact, that is, E ∈AP ⁡ (Ĝ), and for us to have that E∉Cδ∗(G).

3.1 Approximation properties

We recall the classical notion of the Banach space approximation property (see, for example, [15, Section 1.e]). Recall that a Banach space E has the approximation property if, for any Banach space F, any compact operator T : F → E can be norm-approximated by finite-rank operators. In general, an operator which can be approximated by finite-ranks is said to be approximable.

The Banach space L∞(G) always has the approximation property, so in particular, asking that ℛμ, for μ ∈ L∞(G), be compact is the same as asking if we can approximate ℛμ be finite-rank maps. However, we know that V N(G) has the approximation property if and only if V N(G) is amenable as a Banach algebra, which in turn is equivalent to V N(G) being isomorphic to an algebra of the from

⊕ k=1n𝕄 nk ⊗ C(Xk).

It follows that this is equivalent to G being abelian by finite. See [24, Section 6.1] for details. In conclusion, for general groups G, we have no reason to believe that ℛμ being compact is equivalent to ℛμ being approximable.

So perhaps a better definition of μ being “almost periodic” would be to ask for ℛμ to be approximable. We shall show below that if we do ask this, but in the category of operator spaces, then we get 𝔟Cr∗(G), as we might hope. In the remainder of this section, we shall study some stronger forms of “approximable” and show that each of these gives rise to a notion of “almost periodic” leading back to 𝔟Cr∗(G), at least when the group G is amenable.

Firstly, define AP ⁡ (1)(Ĝ) to be the collection of T ∈ V N(G) such that ℛT can be norm-approximated by maps of the form ℛS, where S ∈ V N(G) and ℛS is finite-rank. It follows immediately from Chou’s work that AP ⁡ 1(Ĝ) = 𝔟Cr∗(G). Indeed, by [3, Proposition 2.3], we have that ℛS is finite-rank if and only if S ∈Trig ⁡ (Ĝ). If G is amenable, then ∥ℛS∥ = ∥S∥ for all S ∈ V N(G). So immediately, AP ⁡ 1(Ĝ) = 𝔟Cr∗(G), when G is amenable.

Define AP ⁡ (2)(Ĝ) to be the collection of T ∈ V N(G) such that ℛT can be norm-approximated by finite-rank A(G)-module maps 𝜃 : A(G) → V N(G). That is, maps 𝜃 such that 𝜃(ab) = a ⋅ 𝜃(b) for a,b ∈ A(G). If A(G) has a bounded approximate identity, that is, G is amenable, then a standard argument shows that 𝜃 is of the form ℛS for some S ∈ V N(G). Infact, the following proposition shows that this is true in general, and so AP ⁡ 2(Ĝ) = AP ⁡ 1(Ĝ).

Proposition 3.2. A finite-rank map 𝜃 : A(G) → V N(G) is a module map if and only if 𝜃 = ℛS for some S ∈Trig ⁡ (Ĝ).

Proof. □

4 Operator-space structures

There is now ample evidence that when considering the Fourier algebra A(G), if we view A(G) as a completely-contractive Banach algebra with its canonical operator-space structure, then A(G) behaves much more closely to being the “dual” of L1(G), as compared to viewing A(G) as simply a Banach algebra. See, for example, Ruan’s original paper [23] or Runde’s survey article [25].

We shall assume standard results about operator spaces, as found in [7] or [20]. The important fact for us is that A(G)⊗̂A(G) = A(G × G), where ⊗̂ denotes the operator space projective tensor product. Let T ∈ V N(G), and consider the map ℛT , now treated as a completely bounded map A(G) → V N(G). Now, 𝒞ℬ(A(G),V N(G)) = (A(G)⊗̂A(G))′, and we see that

⟨ℛT (a),b⟩ = ⟨T,ab⟩ = ⟨Δ(T),a ⊗ b⟩(a,b ∈ A(G),

so RT (a) = (a ⊗ id ⁡ )Δ(T). As A(G)⊗̂A(G) = A(G × G), we have

∥ℛT ∥cb = sup ⁡ {|⟨Δ(T),a⟩| : a ∈ A(G × G),∥a∥≤ 1} = ∥Δ(T)∥ = ∥T∥.

We start by showing that operator space versions of AP ⁡ (1) and AP ⁡ (2) allow us to remove the condition that G be amenable.

Let AP ⁡ cb(1)(Ĝ) be the collection of T ∈ V N(G) such that ℛT can be approximated, in the completely bounded norm, by maps of the form ℛS such that ℛS is finite rank. Again, this means that S ∈Trig ⁡ (Ĝ). However, we now have that ∥ℛT −ℛS∥cb = ∥T − S∥, so we immediately conclude that T ∈ Cδ∗(G). Hence AP ⁡ cb(1)(Ĝ) = 𝔟C r∗(G).

Similarly, if we define AP ⁡ cb(2)(Ĝ) to be the collection of T ∈ V N(G) such that ℛT can be approximated, in the completely bounded norm, but finite-rank module maps, then as above, AP ⁡ cb(2)(Ĝ) = AP ⁡ cb(1)(Ĝ) = 𝔟C r∗(G).

These are very strong approximation properties. A much weaker notion is the following. Define AP ⁡ cb(Ĝ) be the the collection of R ∈ V N(G) such that ℛT can be approximated, in the completely bounded norm, by arbitrary finite-rank maps from A(G) to V N(G). From [7, Proposition 8.1.2], it follows that T ∈AP ⁡ cb(Ĝ) if and only if Δ(T) ∈ V N(G)⊗̌V N(G), where here ⊗̌ denotes the operator space injective tensor product. This agrees with the minimal (or spacial) C∗-tensor product (see [7, Proposition 8.1.6]).

Theorem 4.1. Let G be a discrete group. Then AP ⁡ cb(Ĝ) = 𝔟Cr∗(G) = C r∗(G).

Proof. Let (es)s∈G be the standard orthonormal basis of ℓ2(G). Each T ∈ V N(G) is uniquely determined by the vector τ = T(eeG) ∈ ℓ2(G), as T(x) = τ ∗ x for x ∈ ℓ2(G), namely the convolution of τ with x. Under this, we identify V N(G) with the collection of vectors τ ∈ ℓ2(G) such that convolution by τ on the left gives a bounded operators on ℓ2(G). Under this identification, Trig ⁡ (G) is simply the linear span of (es) in ℓ2(G).

Similarly, we identify V N(G × G) with a subspace of ℓ2(G × G) = ℓ2(G) ⊗ ℓ2(G). We can check that if T ∈ V N(G) and τ = T(eeG), then

Δ(T)(e(eG,eG)) = (τsδs,t)(s,t)∈G×G,

where δs,t is the Kronecker delta.

Define U : ℓ2(G) → ℓ2(G × G) and V : ℓ2(G × G) → ℓ2(G) by U(es) = e(s,s) and V (e(s,t)) = δs,tes for s,t ∈ G. Define a map 𝜃 : V N(G × G) → ℓ2(G) as follows. For R ∈ V N(G × G), let ρ = R(e(eG,eG)) ∈ ℓ2(G × G). Then define

𝜃(R) = ρs,ss∈G ∈ ℓ2(G).

Notice that

V RU(es) = V R(e(s,s)) = ∑ t,r∈Gρ(t,r)V (e(ts,rs)) = ∑ t,r∈Gρ(t,r)δt,rets = ∑ t∈Gρ(t,t)ets = 𝜃(R)∗es.

Consequently, convolution on the left by 𝜃(R) is a bounded operator, namely V RU, and so 𝜃(R) induces a member of V N(G). Notice that

V Δ(T)U = T(T ∈ V N(G)).

If T,S ∈ V N(G), say τ = T(eeG) and σ = S(eeG), then

𝜃(T ⊗ S) = τsσss∈G.

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality shows that 𝜃(T ⊗ S) ∈ ℓ1(G) ⊆ ℓ2(G), and so V (T ⊗ S)U is in the closure of Trig ⁡ (Ĝ), that is, in Cr∗(G). By continuity, we conclude that V RU ∈ Cr∗(G) for any R ∈ V N(G)⊗̌V N(G). Hence, if T ∈ V N(G) is such that Δ(T) ∈ V N(G)⊗̌V N(G), then T = V Δ(T)U ∈ Cr∗(G), as required. □

We shall have to work harder to prove this result for arbitrary locally compact groups G.

One approach would be the following. Let Λ be the collection on compact, symmetric neighbourhoods of eG. For T ∈ V N(G), let

xK = T(χK)|K|−1(K ∈ Λ).

Then (xK) is a (in general unbounded) net in L2(G). However, the net (χK|K|−1) K∈Λ is a bounded net in L1(G) which is an approximate identity. In particular, for f ∈ L2(G), we have that f = lim ⁡ K|K|−1χ K ∗ f. As V N(G) commutes with convolution on the right, we have that

T(f) = lim ⁡ KT(χK ∗ f)|K|−1 = lim ⁡ KT(χK) ∗ f|K|−1 = lim ⁡ KxK ∗ f(f ∈ L2(G)),

where the limit is in norm.

Thus (xK) determines T; in fact, if ψ is the left Plancherel weight, then ψ(T∗T) = limsup ⁡ K∥xK∥22. For T ∈ V N(G) and f,g ∈ L2(G), we have

(T∗(f)|g) = (f|T(g)) = lim ⁡ K(f|xK ∗ g) = lim ⁡ K(xK∗∗ f|g).

Hence |K|−1T∗(χ K) might not be xK∗, but the net (xK∗) K∈Λ does induce the operator T∗. Here the involution is that on L1(G),

xK∗(s) = Δ(s−1)x K(s)¯(s ∈ G).

As this is only defined upon L2(G) ∩ L1(G), we technically have to perturb each xK so that it is integrable.

The problem is then to reconstruct (xK) from Δ(T) in some way. For example,

xK ⊗ f = |K|−1T(χ K) ⊗ f = |K|−1WΔ(T)W∗(χ K ⊗ f)(f ∈ L2(G)).

Hence, for f ∈ L2(G), we have that

(xK|f) = lim ⁡ L|L|−1(x K ⊗ χL|f ⊗ χL) = lim ⁡ L|L|−1|K|−1Δ(T)W∗(χ K ⊗ χL)|W∗(f ⊗ χ L).

For each L ∈ Λ, let eL = χL|L|−1∕2, so that ∥eL∥2 = 1. Then, for any R ∈ℬ(L2(G × G)), the following limit exists

ϕK,f(R) = lim ⁡ L∈Λ|K|−1RW∗(χ K ⊗ eL)|W∗(f ⊗ e L),

so that ϕK,f ∈ℬ(L2(G × G))′ with ∥ϕK,f∥≤|K|−1∕2∥f∥ 2. We then have that

(xK|f) = ⟨ϕK,f, Δ(T)⟩(f ∈ L2(G)).

How are we going to do this?

Idea: We can do the above for V N(G × G) as well, of course. The idea, copying what we did in the discrete case, would be to find some bounded operation which takes a “convolution family” given by V N(G × G) and gives back a “convolution family” inducing a member of V N(G). We want this to be an inverse to Δ, and which hopefully does interesting things to V N(G) ⊗ V N(G).
