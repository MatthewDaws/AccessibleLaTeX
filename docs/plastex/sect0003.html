2.1 For the Fourier algebra

We now turn our attention to the Fourier algebra $A(G)$. Let $G$ be a locally compact group. Let $\lambda :G\rightarrow \mathcal B(L^2(G))$ be the left-regular representation

\[ (\lambda (s)f)(t) = f(s^{-1}t) \qquad (f\in L^2(G), s,t\in G). \]

By integrating, we get a contractive homomorphism $\lambda :L^1(G)\rightarrow \mathcal B(L^2(G))$. The norm closure of $\lambda (L^1(G))$ is $C^*_ r(G)$, the reduced group C$^*$-algebra of $G$. If we take the weak-operator topology closure, then we get $VN(G)$, the group von Neumann algebra. Equivalently, $VN(G)$ is the von Neumann algebra generated by $\{ \lambda (s) : s\in G \} $. The predual of $VN(G)$ is $A(G)$, the Fourier algebra of $G$. This algebra is in standard position on $L^2(G)$, and so, for each $a\in A(G)$, there exists $f,g\in L^2(G)$ such that

\[ \langle {T},{a}\rangle = (Tf|g) \qquad (T\in VN(G)). \]

We can treat $a$ as a member of $C_0(G)$ by

\[ a(s) = \langle {\lambda (s)},{a}\rangle = (\lambda (s)f|g) = \int _ G f(s^{-1}t) \overline{g(t)} \ dt \qquad (s\in G). \]

We identify $L^2(G)\otimes L^2(G)$ with $L^2(G\times G)$. Define a unitary $W\in \mathcal B(L^2(G\times G)$ by

\[ (Wf)(s,t) = f(s,st) \qquad (s,t\in G, f\in L^2(G\times G)). \]

Then we may define a map $\Delta :VN(G)\rightarrow VN(G)\overline\otimes VN(G) = VN(G\times G)$ by

\[ \Delta (T) = W^*(T\otimes I)W \qquad (T\in VN(G)). \]

We can check that $\Delta (\lambda (s)) = \lambda (s)\otimes \lambda (s) = \lambda (s,s)$ for $s\in G$. Then $\Delta $ is a normal $*$-homomorphism which, in the appropriate sense, is co-associative. The pre-adjoint of $\Delta $ turns $A(G)$ into a Banach algebra, and we can check that $A(G)$ becomes a (not closed) subalgebra of $C_0(G)$.

There are lots of ways to define the multiplier algebra of a C$^*$-algebra. For us, the most useful approach shall be to follow Pedersen, [ 19 , Section 3.12 ] . If $\mathcal A$ is a C$^*$-algebra inside $\mathcal B(H)$, and $\mathcal M\subseteq \mathcal B(H)$ is the von Neumann algebra generated by $\mathcal A$, then we can identify $M(\mathcal A)$ with

\[ \{ x\in \mathcal M : ax,xa \in \mathcal A \ (a\in \mathcal A) \} . \]

Using this, we can identify $M(C^*_ r(G))$ as a $*$-subalgebra of $VN(G)$. By the definition of the spacial tensor product, we identify $C^*_ r(G)\otimes C^*_ r(G)$ with a $*$-subalgebra of $VN(G)\overline\otimes VN(G) = VN(G\times G)$. Hence, again, we identify $M(C^*_ r(G)\otimes C^*_ r(G))$ with a $*$-subalgebra of $VN(G\times G)$. We can then show that $\Delta $ restricts to give a coassociative, non-degenerate $*$-homomorphism $C^*_ r(G) \rightarrow M(C^*_ r(G) \otimes C^*_ r(G))$, and so $\mathbb G = (C^*_ r(G),\Delta )$ is a quantum semigroup (which obviously also has extra structure). It is simple to see that $\lambda (s) \in M(C^*_ r(G))$ for each $s\in G$. For further details, see [ 13 , Chapter 8 ] , although be aware that Lance looks at the right-regular representation.

Following Chou, [ 3 ] , we define ${\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)$ to be the linear span of $\{ \lambda (s):s\in G\} $ in $M(C^*_ r(G))$, and let $C^*_\delta (G)$ be the closure.

Proposition 2.1. We have that $\Delta $ restricts to $C^*_\delta (G)$ to give a $*$-homomorphism $C^*_\delta (G) \rightarrow C^*_\delta (G)\otimes C^*_\delta (G)$, turning $C^*_\delta (G)$ into a compact quantum group.

Proof

By the definition of the spacial tensor norm, it is clear that $C^*_\delta (G)\otimes C^*_\delta (G)$ is the closure of ${\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)\otimes {\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)$ inside $VN(G)\overline\otimes VN(G) = VN(G\times G)$. It is hence easy to see that $C^*_\delta (G) \otimes C^*_\delta (G)$ can be identified with $C^*_\delta (G\times G)$, from which it follows that $\Delta $ does map $C^*_\delta (G)$ into $C^*_\delta (G) \otimes C^*_\delta (G)$.

Notice that

\[ (\lambda (st^{-1})\otimes I)\Delta (\lambda (t)) = (\lambda (st^{-1})\otimes I)(\lambda (t)\otimes \lambda (t)) = \lambda (s)\otimes \lambda (t) \qquad (s,t\in G). \]

It hence follows that $\{ (a\otimes I)\Delta (b) : a,b\in C^*_\delta (G) \} $ is linearly dense in $C^*_\delta (G) \otimes C^*_\delta (G) = C^*_\delta (G\times G)$. Similarly, $\{ (I\otimes a)\Delta (b) : a,b\in C^*_\delta (G) \} $ is linearly dense in $C^*_\delta (G) \otimes C^*_\delta (G)$, showing that $C^*_\delta (G)$ is a compact quantum group.

We remark that the Haar state $h$ on $C^*_\delta (G)$ is simply the map given by $h(\lambda (s)) = 1$ if $s=e_ G$ the identity of $G$, and $0$ otherwise. Let $G_ d$ be the group $G$ with the discrete topology. If $G_ d$ is amenable, then we know that $C^*_ r(G_ d) = C^*(G_ d)$, and so it follows that $C^*_\delta (G)$ is isomorphic to $C^*_ r(G_ d)$, and in particular, the Haar state is faithful. In general, it seems possible that the Haar state could fail to be faithful. 1

Theorem 2.2. Let $\mathbb G = (C^*_ r(G),\Delta )$ be as above. Then $\mathfrak b\mathbb G = (C^*_\delta (G),\Delta )$.

Proof

By the universal property, we have that $C^*_\delta (G) \subseteq {\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb G)$. We follow Søltan’s construction of ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb G)$; in particular, we shall show that if $T\in \mathcal B(H)\otimes M(C^*_ r(G))$ is a unitary representation, then each matrix element of $T$ is a member of ${\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)$. This shows that ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb G) \subseteq C^*_\delta (G)$, as required.

If $H$ is $n$-dimensional, then we can identify $\mathcal B(H)$ with $\mathbb M_ n$, and so identify $\mathcal B(H)\otimes M(C^*_ r(G))$ with $\mathbb M_ n(M(C^*_ r(G))$. Let $T=(T_{ij})_{1\leq i,j\leq n}$. Then $T$ being a representation implies that

\[ \Delta (T_{ij}) = \sum _{k=1}^ n T_{ik}\otimes T_{kj} \qquad (1\leq i,j\leq n), \]

see before Lemma 2.6 in [ 26 ] . Again, we identify $M(C^*_ r(G))$ with a $*$-subalgebra of $VN(G)$. Now, $VN(G)$ becomes an $A(G)$-module, and we have that

\[ \langle {a\cdot T},{b}\rangle = \langle {T},{ab}\rangle = \langle {\Delta (T)},{a\otimes b}\rangle \qquad (a,b\in A(G), T\in VN(G)). \]

Hence

\[ a\cdot T_{ij} = \sum _{k=1}^ n \langle {T_{ik}},{a}\rangle T_{kj} \qquad (1\leq i,j\leq n, a\in A(G)), \]

showing in particular that the map $A(G)\rightarrow VN(G), a\mapsto a\cdot T_{ij}$ is finite-rank. By Chou’s work, [ 3 , Proposition 2.3 ] , this implies that $T_{ij}\in {\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)$. It is easy to see (compare [ 26 , Lemma 2.6 ] ) that the set of matrix elements of $T$ agrees with the linear span of $\{ T_{ij} : 1\leq i,j\leq n\} $, so we conclude that each matrix element of $T$ is a member of ${\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)$, as required.

Instead of working with $C^*_ r(G)$, one could start with the full C$^*$-algebra $C^*(G)$. Søltan shows that ${\mathbb {AP}}(C^*(G))$ is also equal to the closure of the span of $\{ \lambda (s) : s\in G\} $, but this time inside $M(C^*(G))$.

The following tells us that, informally, there is no “bigger” compact quantum group sitting inside $VN(G)$ than $C^*_\delta (G)$.

Corollary 2.3. Let $G$ be a locally compact group, and let $\mathcal A \subseteq VN(G)$ be a unital $*$-subalgebra such that $\Delta $ restricts to give a map $\mathcal A \rightarrow \mathcal A \otimes \mathcal A$, making $(\mathcal A,\Delta )$ into a compact quantum group. Then $\mathcal A \subseteq C^*_\delta (G)$.

Proof
Let $A$ be the subspace of $\mathcal A$ formed by taking the linear span of matrix elements of finite-dimensional, unitary representations of $\mathcal A$. By [ 30 , Theorem 2.2 ] we know that $A$ is a dense $*$-subalgebra of $\mathcal A$. Examining the above proof, we see immediately that $A \subseteq {\operatorname {Trig}}(\hat G)$, from which it follows that $\mathcal A \subseteq C^*_\delta (G)$.

If we replaced $VN(G)$ by $M(C^*_ r(G))$, then the above corollary would immediately follow from the definition of $\mathfrak b C^*_ r(G)$ as a universal object behaving as a compactification.
