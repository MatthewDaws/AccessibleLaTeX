2 Compact quantum group compactifications

We shall quickly sketch Sołtan’s work in [ 26 ] . Following Sołtan, we shall define a compact quantum semigroup to be a pair $\mathbb S=(\mathcal A,\Phi )$ where $\mathcal A$ is a unital C$^*$-algebra and $\Phi :\mathcal A\rightarrow \mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A$ is a co-associative product. Here, and henceforth, $\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A$ is the minimal or spacial tensor product of $\mathcal A$ with itself. To say that $\Phi $ is a co-associative product means that $\Phi $ is a unital $*$-homomorphism and $(\Phi \otimes \operatorname {id})\Phi = (\operatorname {id}\otimes \Phi )\Phi $.

A compact quantum group is a compact quantum semigroup $\mathbb G=(\mathcal A,\Phi )$ such that the linear span of the either of the sets $\{ (a\otimes I)\Phi (b) : a,b\in \mathcal A\} $ or $\{ (I\otimes a)\Phi (b) : a,b\in \mathcal A\} $ is dense in $\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A$. These density conditions imply a large amount of structure: see [ 30 ] or the survey [ 16 ] for further details. The density conditions can actually be weakened: see [ 17 ] .

Compact quantum groups have a very rich (co)representation theory. For example, every irreducible corepresentation is finite-dimensional. As in the classical case, Søltan’s quantum compactification is stated in terms of finite-dimensional corepresentations. We first need the notion of a quantum semigroup. This is a pair $\mathbb S=(\mathcal A,\Phi )$ where $\mathcal A$ is a C$^*$-algebra (now not assumed unital) and $\Phi :\mathcal A\rightarrow M(\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A)$ is a co-associative, non-degenerate $*$-homomorphism. Here $M(\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A)$ is the multiplier algebra of $\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A$. See [ 13 ] , for example, for further details about multiplier algebras. Recall that to say that $\Phi $ is non-degenerate means that $\Phi (\mathcal A)(\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A)$ is dense in $\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A$. As such, there is a unique extension of $\Phi $ to a $*$-homomorphism $M(\mathcal A)\rightarrow M(\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A)$. Similarly, $\Phi \otimes \operatorname {id}$ and $\operatorname {id}\otimes \Phi $ make sense as maps $M(\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A)\rightarrow M(\mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A\otimes \mathcal A)$, and so it makes sense to speak of $\Phi $ as being co-associative.

We now state the definitions which Søltan makes in [ 26 ] . However, we shall state them in different ways which are more compatible with our Banach algebra approach. Fix a quantum semigroup $\mathbb S=(\mathcal A,\Phi )$. In the usual way, we use $\Phi $ to induce an associative product on $\mathcal A’$, turning $\mathcal A’$ into a Banach algebra, by

\[ \langle {\mu \lambda },{a}\rangle = \langle {\mu \otimes \lambda },{\Phi (a)}\rangle \qquad (a\in \mathcal A, \mu ,\lambda \in \mathcal A’). \]

Let $X$ be a finite-dimensional Banach space, and let $T\in \mathcal B(X)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$. For each $\mu \in \mathcal A’$, let $T_\mu \in \mathcal B(X)$ be the slice map, $T_\mu = (\operatorname {id}\otimes \mu )T$. We say that $T$ is a bounded representation if $T$ is invertible in the algebra $\mathcal B(X)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$, and the map $\mathcal A’\rightarrow \mathcal B(X), \mu \mapsto T_\mu $ is an algebra homomorphism. Notice that we always have the trivial representation, $I_ X\otimes I_{\mathcal A}$. It is easy to construct direct sums of bounded representations, and with a little more effort, tensor products, see [ 26 , Section 2.1 ] .

Let ${\operatorname {ad}}:\mathcal B(X)\rightarrow \mathcal B(X’), S\mapsto S’$ be the adjoint map. For $T\in \mathcal B(X)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$, let $T^ T = ({\operatorname {ad}}\otimes \operatorname {id})T$ be the transpose of $T$. Then a bounded representation $T$ is admissible if $T^ T$ is invertible in $\mathcal B(X’)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$.

When $H$ is a (finite-dimensional) Hilbert space, $\mathcal B(H)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$ becomes a C$^*$-algebra. When a bounded representation $T\in \mathcal B(H)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$ is a unitary, we say that $T$ is a unitary representation. We say that a bounded representation $T\in \mathcal B(X)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$ is similar to a unitary representation if $X$ admits a Hilbert space structure turning $T$ into a unitary representation. Then Søltan shows that admissible representations are similar to unitary representations; the converse need not hold.

Let $T\in \mathcal B(X)\otimes M(\mathcal A)$ be an admissible representation, and let $\lambda \in \mathcal B(X)’$. Then $(\lambda \otimes \operatorname {id})T\in M(\mathcal A)$ is a matrix element of $T$. Søltan shows that the linear span of matrix elements of all admissible representations is a unital $*$-subalgebra of $M(\mathcal A)$, see [ 26 , Proposition 2.12 ] . Let ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)$ be the closure of this algebra, so ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)\subseteq M(\mathcal A)$ is a unital C$^*$-algebra. Let $\Delta _{{\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)}$ be the restriction of $\Phi $ to ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)$. Then $\Delta _{{\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)}$ is a $*$-homomorphism from ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)$ to ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)\otimes {\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)$, and the pair $\mathfrak b\mathbb S = ({\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S),\Delta _{{\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)})$ satisfies the density conditions to become a compact quantum group.

The compact quantum group $\mathfrak b\mathbb S$ satisfies the expected universal property. Let $\chi _{\mathbb S}:{\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)\rightarrow M(\mathcal A)$ be the inclusion map. Then, if $\mathbb G=(\mathcal B,\Phi _\mathcal B)$ is a compact quantum group, and $\theta :\mathcal B\rightarrow M(\mathcal A)$ is a non-degenerate 1 $*$-homomorphism such that $\Phi \circ \theta = (\theta \otimes \theta )\circ \Phi _{\mathcal B}$, there is a unique $*$-homomorphism $\mathfrak b\theta :\mathcal B\rightarrow {\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)$ such that $\chi _{\mathbb S} \circ \mathfrak b\theta = \theta $.

If $S$ is a topological semigroup, $\mathcal B=C_0(S)$, and $\Phi $ is the canonical dualisation of the product on $S$, then ${\mathbb {AP}}(\mathbb S)$ is isomorphic to $C(S^{\operatorname {AP}})$, where $S^{\operatorname {AP}}$ is the classical Bohr compactification of $S$.
